The reason you are learning SQL is to be able to retrieve data you need to analyze from a database without having to ask somebody else in your company to do it for you. Sometimes you will be making the judgment about what data are important to retrieve, while other times your team members or bosses will tell you what data they want you to retrieve.

In either case, you will need to be able to sit in front of a blank query screen and somehow get the data you intend. That experience, emotionally, feels very different from writing queries through the Jupyter interface, because you will not have any text or guidance to draw upon to help you understand how to interpret the database and generate a query.

The Teradata exercises you will complete in this course are designed to serve as a stepping stone between these real-life situations and the Jupyter exercises. It's important for you to experience writing your own queries to answer real business questions with real business data just as you would in a company, and take the time you need to figure out how to arrive at the correct answer, and then check your answers after you have finalized your query. For this reason, you will be given less guidance in the Teradata exercises than in the Jupyter exercises. The goal is to for you to overcome challenges now, before you take on your first analyst job!

Another important opportunity these Teradata exercises provide is the ability to see for yourself what working with “Big Data” feels like. Some questions have been designed so that the queries won’t take too long to run, but as the course progresses and queries become more complicated, you will find it may take minutes for queries to output a result. Teradata is a very fast platform, so these query times represent some of the fastest query times you are likely to see in a business setting. Take advantage of the opportunity to get a feeling for how long different types of queries take to implement.

The best way to become truly proficient with SQL is to practice. To motivate you to practice, the quizzes in this course are primarily based on the answers you retrieve from queries you write to analyze the Dillard’s dataset. For this reason, you will not be given the answers to the Teradata exercises. Instead, you should use the quizzes to determine whether your queries are correct. Don’t worry, though! The Teradata exercises will take you through the steps needed to write queries that will help you answer the quiz questions.

Save your written queries while completing the Teradata exercises so you can use or adapt them to answer quiz questions (you can save the queries in your Teradata account or copy them to a document you save on your computer). Saving the queries gives you a portfolio to show employers, or one you can refer to after completing this course.

Plan to allot a significant amount of time to finish the quizzes each week. Since the quizzes are based on writing actual queries, they take time to complete. More complex queries also take longer to run, so the exercises and quizzes will take longer to complete. We strongly encourage you to use the course discussions and Stack Overflow ( www.stackoverflow.com ) to ask questions and help other people when they have questions as well.

This course is primarily designed to help you gain the technical skill set that will allow you to write SQL queries. However, writing SQL queries is only one part of your job as a data analyst. The other part of your job will be to know your dataset deeply enough to write queries that give correct and meaningful answers so you and your company can make good decisions about what actions to take. Real data are messy, and if you don’t pay attention to the details of the mess, you might execute SQL queries that lead you and your company to incorrect conclusions. We won’t go into depth about how to analytically or physically clean data in this course, but I will ask you to write queries that take some of the abnormalities of the data into account. Doing so will make your queries more complicated, but it is important that you learn to write queries that address imperfections in the data. I encourage you to think about these exercises as an opportunity to learn how to arrive at correct answers, more so than an opportunity to run queries that don’t crash.

Overall Objectives for the Teradata Exercises

Week 2: In Week 2 your aims should be to become comfortable with the Teradata front end interface, and the small differences in MySQL and Teradata SQL syntax. You should also aim to get a good sense of what information the Dillard’s data set contains, and what kind of analysis questions the data set could help you answer. Finish the week knowing how to retrieve, and change the format of data from single tables that meet specified criteria.

Week 3: In Week 3 your aims should be to become very confident in your ability to use the main keywords in a SQL query, and to begin appreciating how long queries take when you are combining data from multiple tables with millions of rows. Finish the week knowing how to summarize data, how to segment summaries of your data, and how to combine data across multiple tables.

Weeks 4/5: In Weeks 4 and 5, your aims should be to become comfortable translating analysis questions into SQL queries, and confident that you can generate creative approaches to retrieving data in formats that are different from the way they are stored in a database. You should also aim to gain a deeper appreciation of how long real queries take to write and to execute. Overall, you want to finish these weeks feeling like a completely independent SQL analyst!
